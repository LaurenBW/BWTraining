Data Storytelling — The 'So What?' Test

Raw data means nothing without context. This activity practises turning data points into a narrative that moves a client conversation forward — using the 'so what?' test on every piece of data before you share it, and building a clear Before / Middle / End story.

Case study
Brightside Legal — Month 1 data. You have your first full month of data and are preparing for the 30-day check-in call with James Wong (Decider). Sarah Chen (Buyer) has been invited but hasn't confirmed attendance. This is your first opportunity to demonstrate value with real numbers.

Part 1 — Worked Example

Below is a complete worked example using Brightside Legal's Month 1 data. Follow each step carefully — the process matters as much as the output.

Step 1 — The raw data points
a.Staff attendance: 96% average across all three staff in Month 1
b.Invoice #1041 is 8 days overdue — first overdue invoice for this client
c.Portal logins: 14 in Month 1 (up from 4 in Week 1)
d.Gong transcript (Week 6 call, 18:42 mark): James mentioned Sarah is considering hiring a 4th person next quarter
e.Priya Nair submitted 3 support queries in Week 1, zero in Weeks 2–4
↓
Step 2 — Apply the 'so what?' test to each
a. 96% attendance:So what? → The team is settled and engaged — a genuine result for three new hires starting simultaneously. This validates the hire and is exactly the kind of data that moves Sarah from sceptical to confident. Worth naming explicitly on the call.
b. Overdue invoice:So what? → First overdue invoice. Not a crisis — but worth raising proactively before it becomes one or before Collections contacts the client directly. This is a relationship conversation, not a collections call. The fact that you're flagging it builds trust rather than eroding it.
c. Portal logins up:So what? → The client is becoming more self-sufficient with the platform. Tripling logins in one month means they're engaging with the data — which means they care. This is an adoption signal, not just a usage number.
d. Hiring comment:So what? → This is a growth signal — and it's the most commercially important data point in this set. A casual comment from James about what Sarah is thinking is an invitation to open a staffing conversation. It should not be mentioned casually. It should be planned for.
e. Priya's queries:So what? → A steep but very short learning curve. Three queries in Week 1, zero since. This tells a story about someone who hit the ground running and figured it out quickly. Worth naming — it says something positive about the quality of the hire and about Priya specifically.
↓
Step 3 — The narrative (Before / Middle / End)
BEFORE — where we were

"When we kicked off six weeks ago, you were onboarding three new team members simultaneously — which is a significant ask. The goal was simple: get great people in, integrated quickly, with minimal disruption to your existing workflow."

MIDDLE — what happened, and why it matters

"Month 1 has been genuinely strong. Attendance across all three staff has averaged 96% — which is a real result for three people who all started at once. Priya in particular had a steep learning curve in Week 1 — she submitted three platform queries — but hasn't raised a single issue since. She's operating independently. Your portal usage has also tripled, from 4 logins in your first week to 14 in Month 1 — which tells me you're getting comfortable with the data and the platform. One thing I do want to flag directly: Invoice #1041 from late last month is currently 8 days overdue. I'd rather raise it now than let it sit — can we get that sorted this week?"

END — what it means and what's next

"The early signs are really positive. The team is settled, the data is clean, and you're engaging with the platform. I also noticed in our last call that you mentioned Sarah is thinking about a fourth hire next quarter — I'd love to get ahead of that conversation with you. Sourcing timelines can be longer than people expect, so if that's still on the cards, it might be worth a separate call to talk through what that role would look like."

What makes this good: Every data point has a 'so what?' before it's included. The narrative has a clear arc. The overdue invoice is raised proactively and framed positively. The growth signal is picked up and turned into a next step — not just acknowledged and dropped. The end does not trail off. It closes with a specific ask.

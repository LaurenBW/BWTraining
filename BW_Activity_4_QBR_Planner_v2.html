Case study
Brightside Legal — Week 10 QBR. Sarah Chen (Buyer) and James Wong (Decider) attending. 3 months of strong performance data. One previously overdue invoice now resolved. Growth signal: 4th hire next quarter. Part-time to full-time conversation to advance. Alex Rivera, AU/NZ shift CSM.

Part 1A — Worked Example: Brightside Legal QBR

PRE-CALL — Relationships check (R)
Who should be in the room?Sarah Chen (Buyer / Managing Partner) + James Wong (Decider / Office Manager). Priya, Marcus, and Lena are not needed — this is a strategic review, not a user session. Having both Sarah and James creates the opportunity to move Sarah's relationship level in the room.
Internal stakeholders to loop in?Finance — Invoice #1041 is resolved but worth referencing proactively to show Alex is on top of it. No other internal stakeholders needed for this call.
Do they know Alex's value?James — yes. Sarah — partially. She knows Alex is the contact, but hasn't experienced Alex as a strategic partner yet. The QBR is the primary moment to change that perception.
Pre-call move with James5-minute call with James the day before: "If there's one thing Sarah would love to see from the data tomorrow, what would it be?" Use his answer to shape the emphasis of the narrative. This demonstrates you know the room before you enter it.
Alex's intention for this callLeave with Sarah at Level 2. Advance the 4th hire conversation. Plant the pt→ft seed. Agree a clear next step. Make the call feel like it was worth Sarah's time — not just a formality.
PRE-CALL — Insights check (I)
Direct data to bringAttendance avg 96% across 3 months (Priya 98%, Marcus 95%, Lena 96%). Portal logins: 4 → 14 → 22 (tripling). Support queries: 3 in Week 1, zero since. Invoice #1041 overdue — resolved after proactive follow-up.
Data passed the 'so what?' test?Attendance: validated the hire and the model. Portal: client is engaging, adoption is building. Support drop-off: Priya is operating independently. Invoice: raised proactively — builds trust rather than eroding it.
Growth signal to advanceJames mentioned at 18:42, Week 6 call: Sarah is considering a 4th hire next quarter. Has not been raised directly with Sarah yet. This QBR is the moment.
What is still missing?No direct feedback from Sarah on overall satisfaction. Will ask on the call: "From your perspective, what's working well — and is there anything you'd want us to do differently?"
ON THE CALL — Structure and strategy (S)
StructureBefore / During / After — chronological. Suits a three-month progress review. Gives Sarah a clear narrative to follow without needing to process raw numbers.
BEFORE — where we started"Three months ago you were onboarding three new team members simultaneously — which is a significant undertaking. The goal was clear: get the right people in, integrated quickly, with minimal disruption to your workflow."
DURING — where we are now"Month by month, the numbers have been really strong — 96% average attendance across the whole team, portal usage tripling from your first week to now, and Priya in particular had a brief learning curve in Week 1 then hasn't raised a single issue since. One invoice was slightly delayed in Month 1 — I flagged it proactively and it was resolved within a few days. The team is settled and delivering."
AFTER — where we go next"Looking ahead — I know you've been thinking about a fourth team member for next quarter. I'd love to get ahead of that conversation now, given that sourcing timelines can be longer than expected. And separately, given how well the current team is performing, it might be worth exploring whether full-time makes more sense commercially than part-time for any of the three roles."
Key question for Sarah (Buyer)"What would a clear win look like for you at the six-month mark?" (Connects to ROI and gives Alex a specific metric to aim for.)
Key question for James (Decider)"Is there anything about how we work together that you'd want to change?" (Opens the door for honest feedback while signalling confidence.)
POST-CALL — Execution (E)
Summary emailWithin 24 hours. Include: key takeaways from the call, agreed next steps with owners, the Gong link, and — if the pt→ft conversation was opened — a brief overview of what full-time rates look like.
HubSpotEvery commitment logged immediately after the call ends. Next follow-up date set. Deal stage updated if the expansion conversation was advanced.
Success metric for this QBRSarah engages with the data and asks at least one question. The 4th hire conversation is opened (not closed — just opened). A clear next step is agreed on the call itself, not left vague.
Pivot plan if it goes off trackIf Sarah is disengaged: shorten data section, ask more questions, make it about her priorities rather than the numbers. If the growth conversation gets deflected: don't push. Plant the seed — "Happy to come back to that whenever you're ready" — and move on. Follow up in the summary email.
What makes this good: Every section connects to a RISE pillar. The pre-call move with James is specific and strategic. The 'so what?' test has been applied to every data point before the call. The growth conversation has a planned moment (the "After" section) — it's not improvised. The post-call actions are immediate and concrete, not vague intentions.

Part 1B — Worked Example: The Annual 5% Salary Increase

This is one of the most commonly mishandled execution workflows at BruntWork. The process is straightforward — notify the client one month ahead, confirm in writing, update Finance. Where it breaks is the pushback conversation. A CSM who has built a strong Relationship with a client handles this cleanly. One who hasn't gets a negotiation.

Brightside Legal — Annual 5% Salary Increase Notification
Brightside Legal's three staff are approaching their 12-month mark. Per the BruntWork standard contract, all remote staff receive a 5% salary increase at 12 months. Alex needs to notify Sarah one month in advance. The relationship with Sarah is now at Level 2 — better than it was, but not fully solidified. Alex prepares for the conversation knowing there may be pushback.

Why this clause exists — know this before you call

The purpose of the annual increase: Staff retention. A BruntWork remote employee who doesn't receive a market-rate annual increase will look elsewhere. If your client's best staff member leaves because BruntWork doesn't keep pace with the market, your client has a worse problem than a 5% cost increase. The annual increase protects the quality of the team your client has already invested time and trust in. Frame it that way — because it's true.

How to open the conversation

Alex's opening — written out in full: "Hi Sarah — following up from our QBR last month. I want to give you advance notice of something that's coming up in [month]: as Priya, Marcus, and Lena approach their 12-month anniversary, their annual salary review is due. Per the standard contract terms, BruntWork applies a 5% increase at the 12-month mark. I'm flagging this now so it's not a surprise and you can factor it into your planning. Happy to walk you through the numbers if that's useful — would a quick call this week or next work?"

Objections and prepared responses

ObjectionPrepared responseTone
"We didn't budget for this.""I understand — that's exactly why I wanted to give you a month's notice rather than flagging it last minute. The increase applies from [date], so you have time to factor it into the current cycle. Would it help if I put together a breakdown of the cost impact so you can share it with your finance team?"Empathetic, practical
"Our staff hasn't performed well enough to justify it.""That's worth discussing — can you tell me more about what you've observed? I want to make sure we're addressing any performance concerns properly. That said, the annual increase is a contractual term and reflects the market cost of retaining quality staff. If there are performance issues, I'd love to work through those separately so we can address them properly."Calm, non-defensive, redirects
"Can we skip it this year?""The increase is a standard contract term, so I'm not able to waive it — but I'm happy to set up a call with my team lead if you'd like to discuss options. What I can tell you is that the increase is what keeps the team stable. Priya in particular has been performing exceptionally — losing her would cost more to replace than the annual adjustment."Firm but warm, leads with retention logic
"This is more than we expected.""Completely understandable — 5% on three salaries adds up. Let me send you the exact figures so you have the full picture. And if it helps, this is also a good moment to revisit whether the current structure — part-time hours — is still the right fit, or whether full-time might actually be more cost-effective per hour for how you're using the team."Transparent, uses it as a strategic moment

After the conversation — what to do next

If accepted: confirm in writing same day. Copy Finance. Update HubSpot with the new rate and confirmation date.

If pushback continues after your prepared responses: escalate to your Team Lead. Do not negotiate the rate yourself — that's above your authority.

If performance concerns are raised: take notes, thank them for raising it, and loop in Staff Services immediately. Performance issues have their own process — they do not override the salary clause.

What makes this good: Alex knows why the clause exists before picking up the phone. The opening is proactive, not reactive. Every objection has a prepared response with a clear tone. The last objection ("more than we expected") is turned into a strategic moment to open the pt→ft conversation. The escalation path is clearly defined.

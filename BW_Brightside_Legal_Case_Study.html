Background

Brightside Legal was referred to BruntWork by another Melbourne-based professional services firm. The Managing Partner, Sarah Chen, had been struggling with the cost of growing her in-house team and was looking for a cost-effective way to handle high-volume paralegal and administrative work without adding full-time headcount.

The firm hired three BruntWork staff simultaneously — an aggressive onboarding approach that created a steep initial learning curve but has largely stabilised. The account was transitioned to you (the CSM) from Sales two weeks after contracts were signed.

Why this account matters strategically

  • Part-time contract with 3 staff — strong expansion potential to full-time if value is demonstrated
  • Sarah mentioned a possible 4th hire in a casual Week 6 comment — an unacted growth signal
  • High-growth sector: legal firms in Australia are increasingly outsourcing paralegal work
  • Referral potential: Sarah is well-networked in the Melbourne legal community

Growth Scenario — Part-time to Full-time Conversion

This section provides the context you need to plan the part-time to full-time conversion conversation in Activity 3. Read it before completing Part 4 of that activity.

Current contract structure

Current arrangement3 BruntWork staff on part-time contracts — 20 hours per week each
Staff membersPriya Nair (paralegal VA), Marcus Obi (bookkeeper), Lena Park (admin assistant)
Full-time equivalent38 hours per week — converting all three would nearly double the account's total contracted hours
Account age at Week 1010 weeks — approaching the point where the team is settled enough for this conversation

Conditions that make the timing right

✓ Team is settled
96%+ attendance across all three months. Priya operating independently since Week 2. No staff complaints or issues raised.
✓ Value is demonstrated
Three months of clean performance data. Portal usage tripling. Client actively engaging with the platform and the relationship.
✓ Client in growth mode
Sarah is already considering a 4th hire. She is not in contraction mode — she is in expansion mode. This is the right climate for the conversation.

Why this is the single biggest FTE opportunity on this account

Converting three part-time staff (20 hrs/week each = 60 total contracted hours) to full-time (38 hrs/week each = 114 total contracted hours) nearly doubles the account's contracted value without any new sourcing, onboarding, or relationship-building required. The team is already performing. The client already trusts the model. The only thing missing is the conversation.

This is also the best moment to have it — before renewal, when the performance data is fresh and the client is already thinking about growth. Waiting until after renewal makes it feel like an upsell. Raising it now makes it feel like strategic advice.

Framing guidance — value-first, not sales-first

Suggested approach for Alex at the Week 10 QBR

"The team has been performing really well over the past three months — 96% attendance, platform adoption growing consistently. Many of our clients find that once the team is settled and delivering at this level, full-time makes more commercial sense than part-time — you get the full benefit of the relationship without the limitations of the hours cap, and it's usually more cost-effective per hour at full-time rates. Is that something worth exploring for any of the three roles?"

When to raise it: In the "After" section of the Before/During/After QBR structure — after the performance data has been presented, when you're talking about what the next quarter looks like. Not at the start (too transactional) and not at the very end (too rushed).

Difficult Conversation — Annual 5% Salary Increase

This section provides the context you need to plan the annual salary increase notification in Activity 4. Read it before completing Part 3 of that activity.

The situation

Contract clauseAll BruntWork remote staff receive a 5% salary increase at their 12-month anniversary — a standard term in all BruntWork contracts
When it appliesPriya, Marcus, and Lena all started simultaneously. Their 12-month mark falls approximately 2 months after the Week 10 QBR
Notification timelineAlex must notify Sarah one month before the increase takes effect — so the notification falls roughly one month after the QBR
Relationship contextBy notification time, the relationship with Sarah should be at Level 2 (post-QBR progress) but is not yet solidified. This conversation will test the quality of the relationship Alex has built

Why this clause exists — understand it before you call

The annual 5% increase is not a revenue mechanism — it is a staff retention mechanism. A BruntWork remote employee who does not receive a market-rate annual review will look for other opportunities. If a client's best-performing staff member leaves because BruntWork has not kept pace with the market, the client has a significantly larger problem than a 5% cost adjustment.

Framing it this way — because it is genuinely true — changes the entire tone of the conversation. You are not delivering a price increase. You are protecting the quality of the team your client has already invested time and trust in building.

The process — what must happen

Step 1Notify the client verbally (call or email) one month before the effective date. Do not send the written confirmation first — the conversation comes before the paperwork.
Step 2Confirm in writing the same day as the verbal notification. Copy Finance.
Step 3Update HubSpot with the new rate and the confirmed effective date.
If escalation neededYou cannot negotiate the rate or agree to a waiver. If a client pushes back beyond your prepared responses, escalate to your Team Lead immediately. Never agree to reduce or waive the increase without explicit TL and Finance sign-off.

Likely objections for Brightside Legal specifically

Sarah is a Managing Partner who is financially careful and has not yet fully committed to the partnership. These are the objections Alex is most likely to face:

ObjectionPrepared responseWhat not to do
"We didn't budget for this.""I understand — that's exactly why I'm flagging it a month ahead. You have time to factor it into the current cycle. Would it help if I sent a breakdown of the cost impact so you can share it with your accounts team?"Don't apologise for the clause. Don't offer to "look into" waiving it.
"The staff haven't performed well enough to justify it.""That's worth discussing seriously — can you tell me more about what you've observed? I want to make sure we address any concerns properly. The increase itself is a contractual term, but any performance issues absolutely need to be worked through — and I'd like to start that conversation now."Don't link the increase to performance. Don't get defensive. Separate the two issues.
"Can we skip it this year?""The increase is a standard contract term, so I'm not able to waive it — but if you'd like to discuss it further, I'm happy to set up a call with my team lead. What I can tell you is that this is what keeps the team stable. Losing Priya, for example, would cost significantly more to resolve than the annual adjustment."Don't agree to skip it. Don't make promises above your authority.
"This is more than we expected.""Let me make sure you have the exact figures — I'll send a clear breakdown after this call. And this might also be a good moment to revisit whether the current part-time structure is still the right fit, or whether full-time might actually be more cost-effective for how you're using the team."Don't hedge on the amount. Use it as a strategic moment to open the pt→ft conversation.
⚠ Critical boundary — do not cross this You cannot negotiate the salary increase rate. You cannot agree to waive or defer it without explicit sign-off from your Team Lead and Finance. If a client is pushing for this, your response is: "I understand your position — let me set up a call with my team lead so we can discuss it properly." Then escalate immediately. Do not try to handle this alone.
